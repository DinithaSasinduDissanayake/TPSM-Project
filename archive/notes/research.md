# Kelaniya Faculty of Science Streams and CoursePathNo Explained

The Faculty of Science at the University of Kelaniya uses abbreviated stream codes for its programs.  These map as follows: 

- **PS (Physical Science):** the general B.Sc. degree in Physical Sciences【29†L107-L112】.  
- **BS (Biological Science):** the general B.Sc. degree in Biological Sciences【29†L107-L112】.  
- **AC (Applied Chemistry):** the B.Sc. Honours in Applied Chemistry (code APCH)【17†L487-L493】.  
- **EC (Electronics and Computer Science):** the B.Sc. Honours in Electronics and Computer Science【32†L67-L75】.  
- **EM (Environmental Conservation & Management):** the B.Sc. degree in Environmental Conservation and Management (code ENCM)【33†L19-L23】.  
- **PE (Physics and Electronics):** the 3‑year B.Sc. in Physics and Electronics【33†L19-L23】.  
- **SE (Software Engineering):** the B.Sc. Honours in Software Engineering【60†L39-L44】.  
- **SS (Sports Science):** the B.Sc. Honours in Sports Science (Sports & Exercise Science)【61†L35-L37】.  
- **IM (Industrial Management):** the B.Sc. Honours in Industrial Management (subject code IMGT)【42†L1-L4】.  

Each of the above codes refers to a specific program/department as confirmed by the Faculty’s handbooks and websites【29†L107-L112】【32†L67-L75】【60†L39-L44】【61†L35-L37】. (For example, the introductory handbook notes that the original B.Sc. program had two streams: “Physical Science (PS)” and “Biological Science (BS)”【29†L107-L112】.)  

1. **What is `CoursePathNo`?**  In Kelaniya’s student system, `CoursePathNo` appears to be an internal identifier for a student’s *subject combination path* or stream specialization.  In the General B.Sc. programs, students first take common modules and then choose a stream (e.g. in Biological Science: Biochemistry, Computer Studies, or Microbiology【16†L435-L438】; in Physical Science: Computer Science, Computer Studies, Electronics, or Statistics【17†L462-L470】).  The `CoursePathNo` likely encodes which of these paths a student is on.  In other words, it reflects the chosen track or subject grouping within a program, not a course number itself.  (The faculty calls these “streams” or “course unit combinations”【16†L435-L438】【17†L462-L470】.)  

2. **What does `CoursePathNo = 0` mean?**  Path number 0 seems to denote the *common or default path* before any specialization is chosen.  For example, all first-year students follow the same compulsory courses (the Faculty notes “all the stream compulsory course units” must be taken in 1st semester of Year 1【64†L439-L442】).  In practice, many students start with `CoursePathNo = 0` for those common modules (or when no specific stream has been assigned yet).  Once a student selects a particular stream in semester 1 or 2, their `CoursePathNo` changes to a nonzero value.  Thus a 0 often just means “no specific path – common/general modules.”  

3. **Why might one student have multiple `CoursePathNo` values in the same year?**  This is expected whenever a student transitions from the general year-1 curriculum into a chosen stream.  For instance, Biological Science students do all of Year 1 Semester 1 as common modules, then “selection of students for preferred subjects in [the] Biological Science streams” happens at the start of Semester 2【15†L429-L437】.  If a student in Year 1 BS took some courses in Sem 1 (with path 0) and then different courses in Sem 2 (with path 1 or 2), the data will show two path numbers in that year.  (Physical Science students pick streams at the very start of Year 1【17†L462-L470】, but even then the first semester common courses might use path 0.)  In short, a student can go from 0→nonzero when they enter their stream, so both values appear in the same year.  

4. **Are blank `CoursePathNo` values meaningful?**  Blank or missing `CoursePathNo` entries generally indicate incomplete or missing data, not a real academic path.  In well-formed records, every course enrollment should have a path (0 or positive).  If it’s blank, it’s safest to treat it as “unknown/missing” and clean it, since the Faculty documentation does not define a null path as special.  

5. **Should a student in PS/BS only have one path per year (with shared common modules)?**  Yes.  By design, each student picks one stream (subject combination) per academic year and then takes the path-specific courses for that stream, along with some common modules.  For example, **all** PS or BS students in Year 1 must take the same set of mandatory courses (the handbook explicitly says all students take the “stream compulsory units” in first-year Sem 1【64†L439-L442】).  Then each student’s chosen stream (e.g. Biochemistry vs. Microbiology in BS) dictates the rest of their courses.  Thus normally a student has one nonzero `CoursePathNo` per year after streams are assigned.  

6. **What about new programs (SE, SS, IM, EC) – do they use paths?**  These newer or specialized programs do **not** use the “multiple path” system in the same way.  Each is essentially a single major with no alternate tracks at intake.  For example, the Software Engineering unit offers only one degree (B.Sc. Honours in Software Engineering【60†L39-L44】).  Sports Science likewise has a fixed Honours program【61†L35-L37】.  Industrial Management (IMGT) is its own specialized program【42†L1-L4】.  The Electronics & Computer Science (ECSC) program is a double-major, but it does not split into separate paths at the time of enrollment – students may later specialize in Electronics or CS as they progress, but not via a `CoursePathNo`.  In practice, these programs will either always use a single path value (e.g. always “1”) or ignore `CoursePathNo` entirely.  

7. **Is “degree + year + path” enough to check timetable clashes?**  No, not completely.  While degree-year-path groups students by most common courses, there are shared modules and electives that cross these boundaries.  For instance, **all** first-year science students (regardless of stream) can enrol in certain general courses (the handbook even notes that every student can take COST 11012 in 1st year【64†L439-L442】).  Also, general electives like ACLT (English literacy) or CMSK (complementary skills) or other cross-stream offerings are open to multiple streams.  Two students in different paths (or even different years) may share common electives or supplementary courses, which a simple degree-year-path key would miss.  Therefore, to reliably catch scheduling conflicts you must consider the actual course enrollments, not just the path grouping.

### What CoursePathNo actually represents
It is the **internal code for a student's subject combination / pathway** (sometimes just called "combination" or "path" in handbooks). 

In real academic terms at the faculty:
- It identifies which specific group of core + compulsory modules a student is following.
- For the big **PS** and **BS** general BSc streams, there are multiple possible subject combinations (e.g., in PS: Math+Physics+Chemistry vs Math+Stats+Physics vs Math+Chemistry+Management Science, etc.). Students get assigned to one after selection (based on performance and quotas).
- In specialised programmes, it points to the sub-specialisation or track (e.g., in BECS: path 1 = Electronics focus, path 2 = Computer Science focus, path 3 = general; in SENG: application domains like Net-Centric, Mobile, Data Science, etc.; in MIT: IS / Business Systems Engineering / Operations & Supply Chain Management combinations).

It's **not** just a random group ID or internal database key — it has real academic meaning: it defines what timetable group / module set the student belongs to. This is exactly why it's useful for your data model.

### CoursePathNo = 0
This almost always means **"no specific path assigned yet"** or **common/general module group**.  
- New first-year students often sit at 0 until subject selection happens (end of first semester or start of second semester for limited-enrolment subjects like COSC, MIBI, BIOC, etc.).
- It can also flag the pure common modules that everyone in the stream takes (ACLT Academic Literacy, CMSK skill development, English/D ELT, etc.).
- Very rarely a data issue, but usually intentional — treat it as the "default / unstreamed" bucket.

### Multiple CoursePathNo values for the same student in the same academic year / stream / year level
Yes, this is **completely expected** and normal. Reasons in real faculty practice:
- Core path + elective/optional modules from a different combination.
- Semester 1 vs Semester 2 (some combinations shift slightly).
- Main degree path + one auxiliary/common path (e.g., a student in PS path X also taking a Management or Computer Studies unit that has its own path code).
- Transition period (e.g., still showing old path while new honours path is being assigned).
- Specialised programmes (SE, MIT, BECS) explicitly have multiple sub-paths/domains that a student can mix in later years.

It is **not** an error — the faculty deliberately allows flexibility in optionals while keeping cores grouped by path.

### Blank CoursePathNo values
Treat these as **incomplete / bad data** in almost all cases.  
They usually mean the enrolment record was created before path assignment or the student is in a transitional state (new intake, change of programme, etc.). In clean production data they should be rare; blanks are not meaningful on their own.

### For PS and BS programmes
Yes — **one student is supposed to belong to exactly one primary real path/subject combination** per academic year (after the selection process).  
However, **many common modules are shared across every path** in the stream (ACLT, CMSK, some English/communication units, certain introductory lectures). This is why the faculty can run large lectures for hundreds of students while still having smaller path-specific practicals/tutorials.

### For newer/specialised programmes (SE, SS, IM/MIT/IT, EC/ENCM, PE, AC, EC/BECS)
They **do use “paths”**, but differently from the old PS/BS style:
- Not broad subject combos — they are narrower specialisation tracks or application domains inside the single degree.
- SE: application domains (Net-Centric, Mobile, Data Science, etc.).
- BECS: Electronics vs Computer Science vs general.
- MIT/IT: IS / BSE / OSCM combinations.
- PE: explicitly Path 1 vs Path 2 in older structures.
- ENCM, AC, SS: usually single or very few paths (more like one cohort).
So the meaning of CoursePathNo is the same (timetable/grouping code), but the faculty uses it more lightly — these programmes have tighter, more fixed structures.

### Detecting timetable clashes: is degree + year + path enough?
**Mostly yes for core modules** — in real life this grouping works very well for the faculty's scheduling (that's why they have the field). Students in the same degree + year + path sit in the same lectures/practicals for their compulsory units.

**But it is not 100% safe** because of:
- Electives/optionals (students can pick across paths).
- Common modules shared across all paths (ACLT, CMSK, etc. — these are deliberately scheduled in non-clashing slots, but still).
- Cross-stream or auxiliary units (e.g., a PS student taking an IMGT or COST unit).
- Later-year honours specialisations or industrial placements.

For accurate clash detection you probably want to go one level deeper: **degree + year + path + actual registered CourseCode list** per student (or at least flag any elective/common modules separately). The faculty itself manages this by having academic advisors and strict pre/co-requisites.

### Rough practical takeaway for your data model
- Keep **CoursePathNo** as a core dimension (it's the faculty's own way of grouping timetables).
- Treat 0 and blanks as special cases (unassigned or common).
- For PS/BS: one primary path per student + shared commons.
- For SE/SS/IM/etc.: path = specialisation track.
- Timetable logic: degree + year + path covers ~80-90% of clashes; add elective logic on top for full accuracy.
